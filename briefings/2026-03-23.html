Exegesis

Psalm 4 is an individual psalm of trust, sometimes classified as an evening prayer — a companion piece to Psalm 3's morning confidence (cf. 3:5, "I lay down and slept"). The superscription's binginôt ("with stringed instruments") places it in liturgical performance. The psalm opens with a bold appeal to the "God of my righteousness" (ʾĕlōhê ṣidqî) — not the psalmist's own merit, but God as the source and vindicator of his righteous standing. The address shifts abruptly in verse 2 from God to human adversaries (bĕnê ʾîš, "sons of men" — possibly social elites), charging them with pursuing rîq (emptiness) and kāzāb (falsehood). The theological pivot comes in verse 3: the verb hiphlâ ("set apart") carries Exodus resonance — God's distinguishing act toward his people. Verse 4's "be angry and do not sin" (rigzû wĕʾal-teḥĕṭāʾû) is genuinely ambiguous: it may be a concession ("even if you are angry, do not sin") or a command to trembling reverence. Paul's quotation in Ephesians 4:26 reads it as concession, but the Hebrew permits either. The closing verse 8 forms a deliberate counterpoint to the communal anxiety of verse 6 ("who will show us some good?"): the psalmist's answer is not prosperity but šālôm — a peace grounded in divine presence, not circumstance. The movement from distress (v. 1) through confrontation (vv. 2–5) to serene trust (vv. 7–8) mirrors the arc of Psalm 3 but resolves more quietly, ending not with battle imagery but with sleep.

SEP — Other Minds

plato.stanford.edu/entries/other-minds/

The problem of other minds is one of epistemology's most persistent puzzles: how do you know that other beings have conscious experiences rather than merely behaving as though they do? John Stuart Mill gave the classic formulation via analogy — I observe my own mental states correlating with behaviors, I observe similar behaviors in others, therefore others likely have similar mental states — but the argument generalizes from a sample size of one, which is inductively feeble. Wittgenstein's criterial approach offered a radical alternative: behavioral and contextual criteria are not merely evidence for mental states but partly constitutive of what it means to ascribe them — pain behavior in appropriate circumstances is what "being in pain" means in our shared language. The inference-to-best-explanation camp (Russell and successors) argues that the hypothesis of other minds simply provides the best explanation for others' complex, adaptive behavior. More recently, simulation theory and theory-theory from cognitive science propose different mechanisms by which we understand others — either by mentally modeling their perspective or by deploying folk-psychological theory. The problem intersects live debates about philosophical zombies (Chalmers), the hard problem of consciousness, animal sentience, and increasingly the question of whether AI systems might have mental states. Wittgenstein's private language argument is particularly relevant: if mental concepts are essentially private, the problem is insoluble, but Wittgenstein argues such privacy is incoherent. For an analytically minded reader, the problem of other minds is a stress test for foundational epistemological commitments — it reveals whether your theory of knowledge can accommodate the most basic fact of social life. The entry engages Mill, Wittgenstein, Russell, Norman Malcolm, Alvin Plantinga, and David Chalmers, among others.

Cognitive Bias — Dunning-Kruger Effect

The Dunning-Kruger effect is the tendency for people with limited competence in a domain to overestimate their ability, while genuine experts tend to underestimate theirs. Kruger and Dunning's 1999 study found that participants scoring in the bottom quartile on logic, grammar, and humor tests estimated their performance near the 62nd percentile — they lacked the metacognitive skill to recognize what they didn't know. You see it in every meeting where the loudest opinion on a technical question comes from the person with the least experience. The corrective is structured: seek calibrated feedback, ask people who actually know the domain to evaluate your work, and treat the feeling of confidence as a signal worth interrogating rather than trusting.
