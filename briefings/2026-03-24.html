Exegesis

Psalm 5 is a morning prayer — explicitly so, with the temporal marker in verse 3 ("in the morning you hear my voice; in the morning I prepare a sacrifice for you and watch") functioning both as liturgical setting and theological claim. The verb ʿārak ("prepare," literally "arrange" or "set in order") is sacrificial vocabulary drawn from the altar cult, and ṣāpâ ("watch") carries the expectant attentiveness of a sentinel. Together they present morning prayer as an offering laid before YHWH, followed by disciplined waiting. The superscription's ʾel-hannĕḥîlôt ("for the flutes") is the only psalm so designated, and the instrument's association with lamentation may color the psalm's tone.

The psalm's structural logic moves through three zones: petition to YHWH (vv. 1–3), a theological meditation on YHWH's character as incompatible with evil (vv. 4–6), and a contrast between the psalmist's access to God through ḥesed ("steadfast love," v. 7) and the enemies' self-destructive deceit (vv. 9–10). The anatomy of wickedness in verse 9 is striking — mouth, inward self (qereb), throat, tongue — mapping a progression from surface speech to interior corruption to the grave itself. Paul quotes this verse in Romans 3:13 as part of his catena proving universal human sinfulness. The imprecatory petition in verse 10, "let them fall by their own counsels," is not vindictiveness but a request that the internal logic of evil be allowed to reach its natural conclusion — a theology of self-destruction rather than divine vengeance imposed from outside. The closing verses (11–12) expand the scope from the individual to the community: those who take refuge (ḥōsîm) in YHWH are covered with rāṣôn ("favor") as with a ṣinnâ (large shield), recalling the shield imagery of Psalm 3:3 and creating a thematic arc across the first cluster of Davidic psalms.

SEP — Free Will

plato.stanford.edu/entries/freewill/

Free will is the capacity of agents to choose among genuinely open alternatives — or so the libertarian position holds. The problem is that this intuitive notion sits uncomfortably with both determinism and indeterminism: if every event is causally necessitated by prior states, it seems no one could have done otherwise; but if some events are random, that hardly grounds the kind of control we associate with responsible agency. Compatibilists — the dominant camp in contemporary analytic philosophy — argue the tension dissolves once "free will" is properly understood not as the ability to have done otherwise in identical circumstances, but as the capacity to act in accordance with one's own desires, reasons, and deliberative processes, absent external coercion. Harry Frankfurt's influential thought experiments showed that moral responsibility can hold even when an agent could not have done otherwise, provided the agent acted on their own endorsed motivations. Incompatibilists divide into libertarians (Robert Kane, Timothy O'Connor), who posit irreducibly agent-causal powers or indeterministic neural processes that ground genuine alternatives, and hard determinists or hard incompatibilists (Derk Pereboom), who accept that free will in the robust sense does not exist and argue we should revise our practices of praise and blame accordingly. P.F. Strawson's 1962 essay "Freedom and Resentment" shifted the debate by arguing that our reactive attitudes — resentment, gratitude, indignation — are constitutive of moral life and cannot be undermined by abstract metaphysical arguments about determinism. Neuroscientific findings, particularly Benjamin Libet's readiness-potential experiments (and their modern successors), have been recruited by skeptics who claim that unconscious neural processes precede conscious decisions, though the interpretation remains hotly contested. The debate is not purely academic: it shapes criminal sentencing, addiction treatment, AI liability, and the coherence of moral education. Whether you think free will is real, illusory, or the wrong question, the entry makes clear that no position comes without significant costs.

Cognitive Bias — Sunk Cost Fallacy

The sunk cost fallacy is your tendency to continue investing in a losing proposition because of what you've already spent, rather than cutting your losses and reallocating. You stay in the bad movie because you paid for the ticket. You hold the declining stock because selling would "make the loss real." The manager keeps funding the failing project because $2 million is already spent and walking away would feel like waste. The economics are clear: sunk costs are irrecoverable regardless of future decisions, so they should be irrelevant to forward-looking choices. The corrective is Munger's: "The iron rule is that you have to be willing to destroy the thing you've built if it's not working. The fact that you put sweat equity in is totally irrelevant."
