Exegesis

Psalm 3 is the first individual lament in the Psalter and the first psalm with a historical superscription, linking it to David's flight from Absalom's coup (2 Samuel 15–18). The superscription is not incidental: it establishes the psalm as a prayer from the nadir of royal power — the anointed king displaced, his own son in rebellion, and his counselor Ahithophel defected. The psalm's three stanzas trace a movement from crisis (vv. 1–2) through confession of trust (vv. 3–4) to serene confidence (vv. 5–6), culminating in a petition and doxological declaration (vv. 7–8). The taunt of the enemies in verse 2 — "there is no yešûʿâ for him in God" — is theologically loaded: yešûʿâ (salvation, deliverance) denotes not abstract rescue but YHWH's concrete intervention in history, and the enemies' claim is that the covenantal relationship has been severed.

The psalmist's response in verse 3 deploys three titles for YHWH — shield (māgēn), glory (kābôd), and "lifter of my head" — each answering the taunt with covenant language. The māgēn echoes Genesis 15:1 where God is Abraham's shield, situating David's crisis within the broader patriarchal promise. The remarkable verse 5 — "I lay down and slept; I woke again" — is often read devotionally, but its force is sharper against the Absalom narrative: to sleep when thousands of enemies surround you is either foolishness or faith, and the psalmist's point is that waking itself is evidence of divine sustenance. The three occurrences of Selah — a liturgical or musical marker whose precise function remains debated — punctuate the psalm at its structural joints, suggesting it was adapted for communal worship. The closing line, "Salvation belongs to the Lord," directly refutes the enemies' taunt of verse 2, forming a theological inclusio: the very yešûʿâ they denied is affirmed as YHWH's exclusive prerogative.

SEP — The Problem of Induction

plato.stanford.edu/entries/induction-problem/

The problem of induction asks what justifies our inference from observed cases to unobserved cases — why, having seen the sun rise every morning, we are entitled to expect it tomorrow. David Hume formulated the challenge in its sharpest form in the Treatise (1739): any attempt to justify induction inductively is circular, and there is no valid deductive argument from past regularities to future ones. Hume concluded that inductive reasoning is grounded in custom or habit, not reason — a result that has unsettled epistemology for nearly three centuries. P.F. Strawson later argued that asking for justification of induction is like asking whether the law is legal: induction simply is what we mean by rational inference from experience, and the demand for justification rests on a confused expectation that induction should meet deductive standards. Hans Reichenbach offered a pragmatic vindication — while we cannot prove induction will work, we can show that if any method will discover regularities in nature, induction will — making it the best available bet. Karl Popper accepted Hume's argument wholesale and argued that science proceeds not by induction but by conjecture and refutation, though critics (notably Wesley Salmon) note that Popper's account smuggles induction back in when scientists choose which unfalsified theories to rely on in practice. Nelson Goodman deepened the problem with his "grue" paradox (1955): define "grue" as green if observed before time t, blue otherwise — all observed emeralds confirm both "all emeralds are green" and "all emeralds are grue," so what determines which projection is legitimate? Goodman's answer — that legitimacy depends on the "entrenchment" of predicates, not the structure of the argument — shifted the question from justifying induction in general to explaining which predicates support lawlike generalizations. Bayesian approaches offer a formal framework through probability updating, but the choice of priors remains contested. The problem matters well beyond philosophy: every predictive model, every extrapolation from data, every argument from precedent implicitly takes a stance on induction — and the problem has never been solved in a way that commands consensus.

Cognitive Bias — Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias is your tendency to seek out, interpret, and remember information in ways that confirm what you already believe — while ignoring or discounting evidence that contradicts it. You see it every day: a manager convinced an employee is underperforming notices every mistake and overlooks every success; an investor bullish on a stock reads the bearish analyst report and finds reasons to dismiss it. Peter Wason demonstrated the effect with his 2-4-6 task: subjects asked to discover a rule almost universally tested only examples consistent with their initial hypothesis, never trying to falsify it. The corrective is deliberate: actively seek the strongest version of the opposing case before concluding you are right. As Munger put it, "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything that I don't know the other side's argument better than they do."
