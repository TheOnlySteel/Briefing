Exegesis

Psalm 4 is an evening prayer of trust — a companion piece to Psalm 3's morning confidence ("I lay down and slept; I woke again"), here closing the day: "In peace I will both lie down and sleep." The psalm is classified as an individual lament, though its tone is more defiant than despairing. The opening appeal to "the God of my righteousness" (ṣidqî) invokes not moral self-congratulation but covenantal standing — the psalmist's right relationship that warrants divine hearing. Verse 4's command — "Be angry, and do not sin" (rigzû wᵉʾal-teḥeṭāʾû) — is notoriously difficult: the imperative rigzû can mean "tremble" or "be agitated" as much as "be angry," and the line may address the psalmist's opponents or his own inner turmoil. The chiastic structure pivots on verse 5's call to trust, flanked by admonitions outward (to adversaries) and inward (to the heart). The theological climax is verse 7: joy that exceeds material abundance — grain and wine being the Deuteronomic markers of covenant blessing. The psalm argues that security is not a function of circumstance but of orientation toward YHWH, an epistemological claim as much as a devotional one.

SEP — Personal Identity

plato.stanford.edu/entries/personal-identity/

The problem of personal identity asks what makes a person at one time the same person at a later time, and what makes a person at any time a person at all. These two questions — persistence and characterization — have generated some of the most enduring debates in Western philosophy, from Locke's memory theory through contemporary analytic metaphysics. The dominant positions divide into psychological continuity theories, which hold that identity over time consists in overlapping chains of memory, intention, and character, and biological or animalist theories, which hold that we are fundamentally human organisms and persist just as any animal does. Locke's original insight — that a prince's soul transplanted into a cobbler's body would yield the prince, not the cobbler — remains the intuition pump driving psychological accounts, though it faces the devastating reduplication problem: if the same psychological states could be copied into two bodies, both would have equal claim to being the original person, yet identity is logically a one-one relation. Derek Parfit famously bit the bullet, arguing that what matters in survival is not identity per se but psychological continuity and connectedness — a conclusion with radical implications for ethics, rational self-interest, and the fear of death. Animalists like Eric Olson counter that psychological theories cannot account for the fact that we were once fetuses with no psychological life, and that any plausible account of our persistence must be continuous with biology. The debate has practical stakes well beyond the seminar room: advance directives, criminal responsibility, the ethics of brain transplants, and — increasingly — the question of whether a sufficiently faithful digital upload would be you. Narrative identity theorists like Marya Schechtman add a further dimension, arguing that what constitutes a person is not a metaphysical relation but the capacity to organize one's life into a coherent self-told story. The entry maps these positions with care, showing how each purchase on the problem generates its own characteristic difficulties, and why the question "Will I survive?" remains one of the hardest in philosophy to answer with any confidence.

Cognitive Bias — Sunk Cost Fallacy

The sunk cost fallacy is your tendency to continue investing in something — a project, a relationship, a bad movie — because of what you've already spent, rather than what you stand to gain going forward. You sit through the last hour of a terrible film because you paid $15 for the ticket, as if leaving would somehow un-spend the money. In business, this is how doomed projects survive budget reviews: "We've already put $2 million into this." Munger called it "the shoe that doesn't fit" — the rational move is always to ask what the next dollar buys, not what the last dollar bought. When you catch yourself arguing from past investment, stop: the money is gone either way, and the only question is whether the future return justifies the future cost.
