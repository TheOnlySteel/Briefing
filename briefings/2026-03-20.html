The Two Ways

1Blessed is the man who walks not in the counsel of the wicked, nor stands in the way of sinners, nor sits in the seat of scoffers; 2but his delight is in the law of the Lord, and on his law he meditates day and night.

Exegesis

Psalm 1 is a Torah psalm — a wisdom composition placed at the gate of the Psalter not because it is chronologically first but because it frames everything that follows as meditation on the life shaped by divine instruction. The architecture is starkly binary: two ways, two destinies, two images. The righteous are a tree (ʿēṣ), planted and fruitful; the wicked are chaff (môṣ), weightless and wind-scattered. The asymmetry is the point — the tree gets four lines of elaboration, the chaff gets one.

The opening ʾašrê ("blessed" or "happy") is not a prayer but a declaration — a wisdom teacher pointing at a life and saying, that one. The three verbs of verse 1 — "walks," "stands," "sits" — trace a descent from casual association to settled identification with wickedness, while the nouns — "counsel," "way," "seat" — mark a progression from ideas to habits to identity. The Hebrew tôrâ in verse 2, often translated "law," carries the broader sense of "instruction" or "teaching"; the psalmist's delight is not in legalism but in a comprehensive orientation toward divine wisdom. The verb hāgâ ("meditates") denotes audible murmuring, not silent reflection — Torah study in the ancient world was bodily and vocal.

The tree image draws on Ancient Near Eastern royal iconography — the king as cosmic tree planted by life-giving waters — but democratizes it: here any person oriented around Torah becomes the flourishing tree. The verb šātal ("planted") implies deliberate placement by another agent; the righteous person is not self-planted but situated by God. The psalm's closing yāda' ("knows") operates in its covenantal register: not mere awareness but intimate, committed relationship. The way of the wicked, by contrast, does not end — it simply perishes (tōʾbēd), dissolves into nothing. The psalm offers no third option.

SEP — Formal Epistemology

plato.stanford.edu/entries/formal-epistemology/

Formal epistemology applies mathematical and logical tools — probability theory, decision theory, modal logic, game theory — to the traditional questions of epistemology: what is knowledge, when is belief justified, and what separates good reasoning from bad. The SEP entry identifies several core programs. Bayesian epistemology, the dominant paradigm, models belief as degrees of credence in the [0,1] interval and uses conditionalization on evidence to update them — a framework that has proven remarkably powerful for modeling scientific reasoning, though it faces challenges from the problem of old evidence and the question of where prior probabilities come from.

Accuracy-first epistemology, a more recent program associated with Joyce and Pettigrew, argues that the fundamental epistemic norm is accuracy — your credences should minimize distance from the truth — and derives probabilism as a consequence rather than an axiom. Formal epistemologists also work on belief revision (the AGM framework for how to rationally change your mind), epistemic logic (what does it mean to "know that you know"?), and the epistemic foundations of game theory (what rational agents can infer about each other's reasoning). The field sits at the boundary between philosophy and mathematics, and its practitioners tend to be comfortable in both departments. Critics from traditional epistemology argue that formalization purchases precision at the cost of philosophical depth — that the hard problems of epistemology (scepticism, the nature of justification, the Gettier problem) resist mathematical treatment. Defenders reply that informal epistemology has spent centuries circling these problems without resolution, and that formal tools at least make the structure of disagreement precise. As the entry notes, formal epistemology is "an expression of the hope that reason can be productively turned upon itself."

Cognitive Bias — Anchoring

Anchoring is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information you encounter when making a judgment, even when that information is irrelevant. Tversky and Kahneman demonstrated this by spinning a wheel of fortune in front of subjects — those who saw the wheel land on 65 estimated the percentage of African countries in the UN as dramatically higher than those who saw it land on 10. The same mechanism operates in salary negotiations (whoever names a number first sets the anchor), real estate (the listing price shapes all subsequent offers), and courtroom sentencing (prosecutors who request higher sentences get them). The insidious thing about anchoring is that knowing about it does not make you immune — even experts anchor, and even arbitrary numbers exert pull. The corrective: before responding to any initial figure, generate your own independent estimate first. The anchor's power is greatest when you have no alternative number in mind.
