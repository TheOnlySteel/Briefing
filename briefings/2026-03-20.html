Exegesis

Psalm 1 is a Torah psalm — a wisdom composition placed at the gateway of the Psalter not because it is chronologically first but because it frames everything that follows as meditation on the life shaped by divine instruction. The architecture is starkly binary: two ways, two destinies, two images. The righteous are a tree (ʿēṣ), planted and fruitful; the wicked are chaff (môṣ), weightless and wind-scattered. The asymmetry is deliberate — the tree receives four lines of elaboration, the chaff a single dismissive clause.

The opening ʾašrê ("blessed" or "happy") is not a prayer but a declaration — a wisdom teacher pointing at a life and saying, that one. The three verbs of verse 1 — "walks," "stands," "sits" — trace a descent from casual association to settled identification with wickedness, while the corresponding nouns — "counsel," "way," "seat" — mark a progression from ideas to habits to identity. The Hebrew tôrâ in verse 2, often flattened to "law," carries the broader sense of "instruction" or "teaching"; the psalmist's delight is not in legalism but in comprehensive orientation toward divine wisdom. The verb hāgâ ("meditates") denotes audible murmuring — Torah study in the ancient world was bodily and vocal, not silent reflection.

The tree image draws on Ancient Near Eastern royal iconography — the king as cosmic tree planted by life-giving waters — but democratizes it: here any person oriented around Torah becomes the flourishing tree. The verb šātal ("planted") implies deliberate placement by another agent; the righteous person is not self-planted but situated by God. The psalm's closing yādaʿ ("knows") operates in its covenantal register: not mere awareness but intimate, committed relationship. The way of the wicked, by contrast, does not end — it simply perishes (tōʾbēd), dissolves into nothing. The psalm presents no third way, no neutral ground — a claim that remains as philosophically bracing as it is theologically uncompromising.

SEP — Formal Epistemology

plato.stanford.edu/entries/formal-epistemology/

Formal epistemology applies mathematical and logical tools — probability theory, decision theory, modal logic, game theory — to the traditional questions of epistemology: what is knowledge, when is belief justified, and what separates good reasoning from bad. The field's dominant paradigm is Bayesian epistemology, which models belief as degrees of credence in the [0,1] interval and updates them via conditionalization on evidence — a framework that has proven remarkably powerful for modeling scientific reasoning, though it faces persistent challenges from the problem of old evidence and the question of where prior probabilities come from. Accuracy-first epistemology, a more recent program associated with James Joyce and Richard Pettigrew, takes a different route: it argues that the fundamental epistemic norm is accuracy — your credences should minimize distance from the truth — and derives probabilism as a consequence rather than an axiom. This reverses the traditional explanatory order and has generated a productive cottage industry of scoring-rule arguments.

Beyond probabilistic frameworks, formal epistemologists work on belief revision through the AGM framework (named for Alchourrón, Gärdenfors, and Makinson), which axiomatizes how a rational agent should change their beliefs when confronted with new information that contradicts what they previously held. Epistemic logic — the formal study of what it means to "know that you know" — provides another strand, raising questions about the depth and limits of introspective access. The field also investigates the epistemic foundations of game theory, asking what rational agents can infer about each other's reasoning under conditions of strategic interaction. Formal epistemology sits at the boundary between philosophy and mathematics, and its practitioners tend to be comfortable in both departments. Critics from traditional epistemology argue that formalization purchases precision at the cost of philosophical depth — that the genuinely hard problems (scepticism, the nature of justification, Gettier cases) resist mathematical treatment. Defenders reply that informal epistemology has spent centuries circling these problems without resolution, and that formal tools at least make the structure of disagreement precise. As the entry notes, the field embodies "the hope that reason can be productively turned upon itself."

Cognitive Bias — Anchoring

Anchoring is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information you encounter when making a judgment, even when that information is arbitrary or irrelevant. Tversky and Kahneman demonstrated this by spinning a rigged wheel of fortune in front of subjects — those who saw it land on 65 estimated the percentage of African countries in the UN as dramatically higher than those who saw it land on 10, despite the number being visibly random. The same mechanism operates everywhere consequential decisions are made: in salary negotiations, whoever names a number first sets the gravitational center; in real estate, the listing price shapes all subsequent offers regardless of the property's actual value; in courtrooms, prosecutors who request higher sentences systematically get them. The insidious thing about anchoring is that expertise provides no immunity — even seasoned professionals anchor, and even numbers you know to be irrelevant exert measurable pull on your estimates. The corrective is deceptively simple: before responding to any initial figure, generate your own independent estimate first. The anchor's power is greatest when you have no alternative number in mind.
