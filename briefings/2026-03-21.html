The Two Ways

1Blessed is the man who walks not in the counsel of the wicked, nor stands in the way of sinners, nor sits in the seat of scoffers; 2but his delight is in the law of the Lord, and on his law he meditates day and night.

Exegesis

Psalm 1 is a Torah psalm — a wisdom composition that functions as the editorial gateway to the entire Psalter, placed first not because it is oldest but because it frames everything that follows as an extended meditation on the life shaped by God's instruction. The psalm's architecture is binary: two ways, two outcomes, two images. The righteous are a tree (ʿēṣ), planted and fruitful; the wicked are chaff (môṣ), weightless and wind-scattered. The asymmetry is deliberate — the tree gets four lines of elaboration, the chaff gets one. The psalmist is not interested in equal time.

The opening word, ʾašrê ("blessed" or "happy"), is not a prayer but a declaration — a wisdom teacher pointing at a life and saying, that one. The three verbs of verse 1 — "walks," "stands," "sits" — trace a progression from casual association to settled identification with wickedness, while the shift from "counsel" to "way" to "seat" marks a movement from ideas to habits to identity. The Hebrew tôrâ in verse 2, often translated "law," carries the broader sense of "instruction" or "teaching" — the psalmist's delight is not in legalism but in a comprehensive orientation toward divine wisdom. The verb hāgâ ("meditates") denotes audible murmuring, not silent reflection; Torah study in the ancient world was a bodily, vocal practice.

The tree image draws on Ancient Near Eastern royal iconography — the king as cosmic tree planted by life-giving waters — but democratizes it: here any person who orients around Torah becomes the flourishing tree. The verb šātal ("planted") implies deliberate placement by another agent; the righteous person is not self-planted but situated by God. The psalm's closing line — "the Lord knows (yôdēaʿ) the way of the righteous" — uses yāda' in its covenantal sense: not mere awareness but intimate, committed relationship. The way of the wicked, by contrast, does not end — it simply perishes (tōʾbēd), dissolves into nothing. The psalm offers no third option.

SEP — The Problem of Evil

plato.stanford.edu/entries/evil/

The problem of evil is the oldest and most persistent challenge to theism: if God is omnipotent, omniscient, and perfectly good, why does evil exist? The SEP entry distinguishes the logical problem (evil and God are logically incompatible) from the evidential problem (the amount and distribution of evil make God's existence unlikely, even if not strictly impossible). Alvin Plantinga's Free Will Defense is widely credited with resolving the logical version — he showed it is possible that God could not create free creatures who never choose evil, making the coexistence of God and evil logically consistent. The evidential problem, sharpened by William Rowe's argument from gratuitous suffering (a fawn dying slowly in a forest fire with no observer and no greater good served), proves harder to dispatch.

Theodicies — attempts to explain why God permits evil — fall into several families: soul-making (John Hick: suffering is necessary for moral development), free will (Augustine, Plantinga: evil is the price of genuine agency), and natural law (evil is a byproduct of a law-governed universe that makes rational action possible). Skeptical theism, associated with William Alston and Michael Bergmann, takes a different tack: we should not expect to understand God's reasons, and our inability to see a justification for some evil does not mean none exists. Critics reply that this undercuts all moral reasoning — if we cannot trust our judgement that pointless suffering is bad, we cannot trust any moral intuition at all. The entry notes that the problem of evil has generated some of the most technically sophisticated work in analytic philosophy of religion, and that the debate has shifted from whether God and evil are compatible to whether the specific patterns of suffering we observe are best explained by theism or naturalism.

Cognitive Bias — Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information in ways that confirm what you already believe — and to ignore or discount evidence that contradicts it. You suspect a colleague is underperforming, and suddenly every missed deadline confirms it while their successful deliveries barely register. Wason's 2-4-6 task demonstrated this in the lab: subjects tested their hypotheses by looking for confirming examples rather than trying to falsify them, and most never discovered the actual rule. The bias is compounded by motivated reasoning — the more emotionally invested you are in a belief, the more aggressively you filter for confirmation. The corrective is simple to state and difficult to practice: actively seek out the strongest version of the opposing view before concluding you're right. As Munger put it, "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything that I don't know the other side's argument better than they do."
