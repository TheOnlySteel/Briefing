Exegesis

Psalm 2 is a royal psalm — almost certainly composed for the enthronement of a Davidic king, though its placement alongside Psalm 1 as a joint introduction to the Psalter signals that the editors intended a broader hermeneutical function. The four stanzas progress through four voices: the narrator's incredulous question at the nations' conspiracy (vv. 1–3), YHWH's derisive response from heaven (vv. 4–6), the king's recitation of the divine decree (vv. 7–9), and a wisdom teacher's admonition to the rebellious rulers (vv. 10–12). The decree formula in verse 7 — "You are my Son; today I have begotten you" — employs Ancient Near Eastern adoption language standard in royal ideology: the Davidic king becomes YHWH's son not by ontological generation but by covenantal appointment at the moment of enthronement, echoing the dynastic promise of 2 Samuel 7:14.

The Hebrew nāsak in verse 6 ("I have set my King") is unusual — its primary sense is "to pour out," suggesting a libation or anointing rite that reinforces the liturgical setting. The verb rāʿaʿ in verse 9 admits an important textual ambiguity: vocalized as tirrōʿēm it means "you shall break them," but as tirʿēm (from rāʿāh) it means "you shall shepherd them" — an ambiguity the Septuagint exploited by rendering it poimaneis ("you shall shepherd"), a reading that enters the New Testament at Revelation 2:27 and 12:5. The psalm's closing ʾašrê ("blessed") forms an inclusio with Psalm 1:1, binding the two psalms into a single prologue: the Torah-shaped life of Psalm 1 and the cosmic kingship of Psalm 2 together frame the entire Psalter as simultaneously wisdom literature and royal theology.

SEP — Underdetermination of Scientific Theory

plato.stanford.edu/entries/scientific-underdetermination/

The underdetermination of scientific theory is the thesis that the evidence available at any given time may be insufficient to determine which of several rival theories is correct — a claim that has served as one of the central challenges to scientific realism since the early twentieth century. The problem takes two forms. Holist underdetermination, associated with Pierre Duhem (1861–1916) and radicalized by W.V.O. Quine, holds that hypotheses are never tested in isolation: a failed prediction tells us something in the bundle of theory plus auxiliary assumptions is wrong, but not which part. A scientist confronted with a recalcitrant observation can always save the core theory by adjusting background assumptions — a point Quine extended in "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1951) to all of knowledge, arguing that our statements about the external world "face the tribunal of sense experience not individually but only as a corporate body."

Contrastive underdetermination makes the stronger claim that for any theory, empirically equivalent rivals can always be constructed — algorithms exist for generating a theory T* that yields all the same observational predictions as T but posits a different unobservable structure. Larry Laudan and Jarrett Leplin mounted the most influential response, arguing that genuine empirical equivalence is transient: as auxiliary knowledge changes, previously equivalent theories diverge in their predictions, and empirical equivalence does not entail evidential equivalence since explanatory virtues bear on theory choice. The debate cuts to the heart of scientific realism: if theories are underdetermined by evidence, what grounds do we have for believing our best theories describe unobservable reality? Realists respond with the "no miracles" argument — it would be miraculous if our theories were empirically successful without being at least approximately true — while constructive empiricists like Bas van Fraassen maintain that the aim of science is empirical adequacy, not truth about unobservables. The entry is valuable for anyone who makes decisions under uncertainty, because the core insight generalizes: in any domain where you choose between competing explanations — medicine, policy, investment — the data rarely compel a single conclusion, and the criteria you use to break ties (simplicity, scope, fertility) may be pragmatic virtues rather than truth-tracking ones.

Cognitive Bias — Availability Heuristic

The availability heuristic is your tendency to judge the probability of an event by how easily examples come to mind — not by actual frequency. When you overestimate the risk of shark attacks or plane crashes, you are substituting the vividness of memorable news coverage for base-rate statistics. The same mechanism makes managers overweight the last quarter's performance in annual reviews, investors pile into whatever sector generated the most dramatic recent headlines, and entire populations misjudge which diseases are likeliest to kill them. Kahneman and Tversky demonstrated that people overwhelmingly believe more English words start with "r" than have "r" as the third letter — because starting letters are easier to retrieve from memory, not because they are more common. The corrective is unglamorous but effective: when estimating the likelihood of something, force yourself to consult the base rate before trusting your gut. Your memory is an echo chamber for salience, not a census bureau.
